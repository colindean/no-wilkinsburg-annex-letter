This is your letter:

8 November 2016

Dear Legislator:

I write to inform you of my experience voting today at my precinct.

I waited in line for a long time. This wait time was frustrating.

I felt intimitated while casting my vote.

I was blocked from entering my voting facility.

I demand that you to take action to ensure that no voter is disenfranchised. Citizens' voices must be heard and any legislator who prevents someone from casting their vote should be removed from office. Any employer who prevents an employee from voting should be punished.

Sincerely,

A concerned voter

Thank you

Your vote, regardless for whom it was cast, is vital to a functioning democracy in our constitutional republic.

If you weren't able to vote, and if there is still time for you to vote, please try again and do not let anyone except a judge of elections or election constable stop you. Demand and write down the names of the people who prevent you from voting and call 866-OUR-VOTE to report it. Remember, though, that many states now require photographic identification.

If you weren't able to vote, you were disenfranchised and you really should call 866-OUR-VOTE to report it in addition to sending this letter. Read more on the ACLU's What To Do When Faced With Voter Intimidation.

The authors of this tool have their political preferences, but feel strongly that any voter who has been nearly or completely disenfranchised must be enabled to tell their elected officials about their problem.

Things to remember

Be factual.

Do not lie. Do not exaggerate. Simply state what happened.

Re-read anything you have written.

Sending something is better than nothing, but something short and grammatically correct is will give the best impression.

Sign your letter.
Your signature means that a person took the time to send this letter. Use a blue pen or some other color for added effect.
